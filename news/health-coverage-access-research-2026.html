Health Coverage Access: What Recent Research Reveals About Options Hiding in Plain Sight

A growing body of research suggests millions of Americans may have access to affordable health coverage they haven't explored—and the gap between available options and actual enrollment continues to widen.

There's a paradox at the heart of American healthcare: while the number of available health coverage options has expanded in recent years, millions of eligible Americans still haven't explored what's available to them. Researchers call it the "coverage access gap"—the distance between the plans people could potentially enroll in and the plans they actually know about.

A 2026 analysis from Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health found that marketplace enrollment increased significantly when enhanced subsidies were available, suggesting that affordability—not lack of interest—is the primary barrier. Yet even as options have expanded, awareness hasn't kept pace. Many Americans who gave up looking for affordable coverage years ago may not realize that the landscape has changed.

Research Finding: According to a recent Gallup survey, approximately one-third of Americans—over 82 million people—have made financial trade-offs such as cutting back on daily expenses or prolonging prescription doses to afford healthcare costs.

The Access Gap: What Research Shows

The concept of a "coverage access gap" has gained attention among health policy researchers in recent years. It describes a situation where eligible individuals don't enroll in available coverage—not because they don't want it, but because they don't know it exists, assume they won't qualify, or find the process too daunting to navigate.

Several factors contribute to this gap:

  • Information asymmetry: The health insurance marketplace is complex, and many Americans rely on outdated assumptions about what's available and what they might qualify for
  • Previous negative experiences: Those who looked into coverage in past years and found it unaffordable may not revisit their options, even as eligibility criteria and plan offerings change
  • Process friction: The perceived difficulty of comparing plans, understanding terminology, and completing enrollment discourages many from starting
  • Life transitions: Major life changes—job loss, divorce, aging off a parent's plan—often coincide with periods of stress when researching insurance feels overwhelming

"The biggest barrier isn't the availability of plans—it's the awareness that affordable options may exist," explains Dr. Sarah Whitfield, a health policy researcher at Georgetown University. "We consistently find that when people actually look at what's available, many are surprised by what they find."

What's Changed in the Coverage Landscape

The health insurance marketplace has evolved considerably over the past several years. Understanding these changes is important for anyone who hasn't reviewed their options recently:

Expanded Plan Variety

The marketplace now offers plans across multiple coverage tiers, from high-deductible Bronze plans designed for those who primarily want catastrophic protection, to comprehensive Gold and Platinum plans for those with ongoing healthcare needs. This variety means there may be options at price points that weren't available in previous enrollment periods.

Improved Comparison Tools

Online platforms have made it significantly easier to compare plans based on specific criteria—including monthly premium, deductible, copays, prescription coverage, and provider networks. What once required hours of research can now often be accomplished in minutes.

Subsidy and Eligibility Adjustments

Income thresholds for premium tax credits have been adjusted over time, and the rules governing eligibility continue to evolve. Americans who checked their eligibility in previous years may find that their situation has changed—particularly if their income, household size, or employment status has shifted.

Who May Benefit Most From Checking

While anyone without adequate health coverage may benefit from exploring their options, researchers have identified several groups that are particularly likely to find affordable health coverage options they didn't know about:

  • Self-employed individuals and freelancers who don't have access to employer-sponsored coverage
  • Adults between jobs or transitioning careers who may qualify for special enrollment periods
  • Early retirees (under 65) who are too young for Medicare but no longer have employer coverage
  • Part-time workers whose employers don't offer health benefits
  • Families with changing incomes who may now fall within subsidy eligibility ranges
  • Young adults turning 26 who are aging off their parents' insurance plans

The Cost of Not Looking

The financial consequences of going without adequate health coverage can be severe. Research from the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau shows that approximately 100 million Americans carry some form of medical debt, and medical bills remain the leading cause of personal bankruptcy filings in the United States.

By the Numbers: Average family healthcare spending now exceeds $24,000 annually, and employer-sponsored health insurance premiums increased by 7.2% over the past year—far outpacing wage growth and inflation.

Even for those who consider themselves healthy, a single unexpected medical event—an emergency room visit, a broken bone, an appendectomy—can generate bills of $10,000 or more without insurance. The financial protection that even a basic health plan provides can be the difference between a manageable expense and a financial crisis.
